# The Front Door — Cross-Product Principle

*Companion to CLAUDE.md and the For-Others Layer doc. Applies across the line
(Finxiety, Mortalia-for-others, and any future tool). Definition only.*

---

## The principle

**The user enters through their question, not through the engine.**

Incumbents (Boldin, ProjectionLab, Addepar) are organized around the *machinery*:
here are your inputs, here are your scenarios, go assemble the journey yourself. The
user must already understand how the sausage is made to ask anything. That forces a
capable person down a long workflow and an anxious person away entirely.

This line inverts it. The person states their **real question in their own words**.
The tool gathers only what *that* question needs, runs the workflow **hidden behind the
curtain**, and returns an answer **shaped like the question they asked**.

> The question is the door. The engine is the room behind it they never have to see.

Key technical insight: most of these life questions resolve to **nearly the same
analysis underneath** — the same sequencing, tax, and drawdown math. The difference is
which question you walked in with and what order the engine runs in behind the scenes.
So one engine can serve many doors.

---

## How the door differs per product

The architecture is shared; the doorway is tuned to the user's state at entry.

**Finxiety — the acute-moment door.**
Entry state: anxiety, a bind, a need for a solution *right now*. The interaction must be
**very short** — an anxious person cannot tolerate a journey. The door is an emotional/
situational statement: "I just got hit with a bill I can't cover," "can I make rent this
month." Get to relief and a first step fast. (See the philanthropy-line identity: the
screen does what a calm person beside you would do.)

**Mortalia (for others) — the life-question door.**
Entry state: a capable person with a plain-language decision. The door is a life question:
"can I safely retire?", "how do I afford my child's college?". Behind it, the engine
gathers what that specific question needs and walks them through it. Same analysis family,
shaped to the question asked.

**Both share:** plain-language entry, hidden workflow, answer in the shape of the question,
short interaction, come-in-and-out tempo.

## Why this is a real (if temporary) edge

Boldin and ProjectionLab will bolt AI and natural-language entry on top — they already
are. But their entire product, pricing, and user mental model is **built around the
sandbox**. Bolting a question-front-door onto a sandbox is awkward: they can't cheaply
un-build the 250 inputs they sell *as* thoroughness. This line is question-first from the
foundation.

That's the desktop-publishing pattern: the incumbent's strength (depth, options, control)
becomes the weight that slows them when value migrates to "just answer my question." It's
not a feature they lack — it's an architecture they can't cheaply reverse. Build the moat
into the foundation, not as a feature on top.

---

## Pricing and positioning — premium, not cheap

**Do not anchor on "cheap" or "$3."** Price is not the differentiator; the question-first
experience is. Leading with cheapness invites a race to the bottom and teaches the user the
value is low.

- **What they pay for:** "I asked my real question and got an answer I trust, in a few
  minutes" — which Boldin cannot deliver at any price right now.
- **Low cost to run is a *consequence*** of stripping the sandbox nobody needed, not the
  pitch. You can pass savings down without making cheapness the story.
- **Model: the annual checkup.** Not a calculator you buy once — a relationship you return
  to at the moments that matter (a Sunday-night conversation, a job change, a market scare,
  the year before retirement, a college decision). Return-on-demand. Premium *because* it's
  brief and trusted and there when you need it, not despite being brief.

This sits cleanly with the **tempo-not-streak** principle: success is the user returning at
a decision/need moment, not daily use. The checkup framing *is* the business-model
expression of titration.

---

## Branding consistency (keep the nervous system intact)

The front door must speak in the same voice as the rest of the line: **you ask in your own
words, it answers in your own words.** No jargon at the threshold. The door is where the
"friend who got out and came back" persona is most visible — it's the moment of first
contact, so it carries the most trust weight.

All prior principles still bind at the door:
- **Honest calibration** — the answer can be "you're clearly fine, don't overthink it" or
  "this matters, here's the rough shape, now get the exact number before you act."
- **Trust line** — correct where it matters, honest about where it's coarse.
- **Input minimalism** — the door asks the fewest questions the answer needs, never 250.
- **Put-it-down design** — the interaction is built to end cleanly, not to retain.
