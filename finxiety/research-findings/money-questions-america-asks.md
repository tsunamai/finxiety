# What Keeps America Up at Night: A Map of the Money Questions Americans Actually Ask

*A research synthesis of search behavior, survey data, and behavioral economics — organized so the questions can be sorted into tools that are buildable vs. not.*

---

## 0. How to read this paper

This is built to answer three things in sequence:

1. **What are people actually asking about money?** (the demand signal — grounded in search volume, Reddit/forum behavior, and survey data)
2. **How should those questions be organized?** (the framework question you raised: socioeconomic vs. event vs. age vs. gender — answered below, and the answer is "none of those as the *primary* axis")
3. **Where do people currently go for answers, and what gap does each channel leave?** (the friends → YouTube → Reddit → government → paid-software → advisor ladder)

The throughline: a money question is rarely just an information request. It's an emotional event wearing an informational costume. Research cited below finds that **how people *feel* about their finances predicts distress roughly 20× more strongly than their actual bank balance** (Anvari-Clark, via Northwestern Mutual / Harbor Mental Health summary, 2025). That single fact reframes the entire design problem: the deliverable people want is *relief and orientation*, and the question is the doorway to it.

---

## 1. The macro picture: the anxiety is near-universal and rising

The "keeping America up at night" framing is, by the numbers, almost literally true.

- **Financial anxiety is now the majority condition.** A long-running employer-benefits study found financial anxiety climbed from **71% (2022) to 90% (2025)**, with the top stressors being inflation (65%), credit card debt (40%), and housing costs (31%) (NAPA-Net / employee financial wellness report, Dec 2025).
- **It's a health and relationship problem, not just a wallet problem.** Northwestern Mutual's 2025 Planning & Progress Study found **~69% of Americans say financial uncertainty has made them feel depressed and anxious**, up 8 points year-over-year. Three-quarters of Millennials and ~71% of Gen Z in serious relationships say money worries have strained the relationship.
- **It disrupts sleep and work.** An AMFM Healthcare national survey (July 2025) found 87% feel anxious about finances, 70% experience that anxiety more than once a week, 77% report disrupted sleep, and ~60% report worse work performance.
- **Self-worth gets entangled with net worth.** A WalletHub survey (Oct 2025) found 69% feel insecure about their finances and **44% believe their financial status defines their self-worth**.
- **Money is the last great taboo.** A 2024 Bankrate survey found Americans consider money *more* taboo to discuss than politics, religion, or weight. Empower found **62% would rather not discuss money** — preferring the DMV line, jury duty, or a spoiled TV show to telling a stranger their net worth.

**Implication for product:** the size of the market is "almost everyone," but the binding constraint isn't lack of information — it's avoidance, shame, and disorientation. The product job is to make the first question askable.

## 2. The master list: what people actually ask

There is no single authoritative ranking, so this triangulates three independent signals. Where they agree, confidence is high.

### 2a. Ranked by raw search volume (Empower analysis of Ahrefs data, U.S., 2024)

These are the literal top "how-to" money searches — and they're humbling in how *basic* they are:

1. **How do I write a check?** — ~187,000/mo (the single most-asked money how-to)
2. **How can I make money online?** — ~107,000/mo ("how to make money" 67k, "...fast" 43k, "how to get rich" 16k)
3. **How do I invest in stocks?** — ~32,000/mo ("how to invest" 18k)
4. **How can I save money?** — ~31,000/mo
5. **How do I freeze my credit?** — ~28,000/mo ("how to build credit" 24k, "check your credit score" 20k)
6. **How can I buy a house?** — ~24,000/mo
7. **How do I file taxes?**
8. **How do I fill out a money order?** — ~19,000/mo
9. **How do I negotiate salary?** — ~17,000/mo
10. **How do I open a Roth IRA?** — ~15,000/mo

Adjacent high-volume signals reported elsewhere: **"Where's my refund?"** is one of the highest-volume money queries in the country (Semrush, 2025), and **"net worth by age"** out-searches "how to eat healthy."

> **The pattern that matters:** the top of the list is split between *transactional literacy* ("how do I do this basic thing") and *orientation/comparison* ("am I where I should be — net worth by age, where's my refund"). Very different emotional registers, both massive.

### 2b. What surveys say people are *worried* about (vs. searching)

Searches skew toward concrete tasks; surveys reveal the worries underneath:

- **Retirement adequacy** — "not having enough to retire" tops the list of future worries (68%, Capital One / The Decision Lab "Mind over Money").
- **Keeping up with cost of living / inflation** (56%).
- **Managing / paying down debt** (45%; credit card debt is the #2 stressor nationally).
- **Affording a home** (saving for or being unable to afford one — ~21%, Bankrate).
- **Income stability / job security** (30% cite no stable income; 21% job security, Bankrate).
- **Emergency resilience** — the canonical "can I cover a $400 surprise expense" question (Fed SHED; ~13–38% can't, depending on methodology).

### 2c. What people ask each other (Reddit / forum behavior)

r/personalfinance (~15M+ members) surfaces recurring archetypal threads that pure search data misses because they're *situational and embarrassing*:

- "Should I save or pay off debt first?" (the perennial #1 debate)
- "I just came into money / inheritance / a windfall — what do I do?"
- "Someone I love died — how do I handle the finances?"
- "I'm 30 and have nothing saved — am I screwed?"
- "Is renting throwing money away, or is buying a trap?"
- "I have two job offers / I want to negotiate — what am I worth?"
- "What's the dumb money mistake I keep repeating?"

These are the questions people **won't put in a Google box with their name attached** but will post anonymously. That anonymity gap is a product opportunity in itself.

## 3. The categorization problem — and the framework that actually works

You asked whether the right organizing axis is socioeconomic, event-based, age-based, or gender-based. **Here is the core finding of this paper:**

> Those four are **modifier dimensions**, not the primary taxonomy. They change the *urgency, flavor, and stakes* of a question — but the same underlying question ("am I going to be okay?") shows up across all of them. If you organize *primarily* by demographic, you fragment one product into many and lose the shared engine. The primary axis that maps cleanly to tools is **the job the person is trying to do**, expressed as a **question type**.

### Layer 1 — The universal jobs (the emotional substrate)

Almost every money question collapses into one of six underlying jobs. This is the behavioral-economics layer, and it's demographic-agnostic:

| # | The job (how it's felt) | The question behind the question |
|---|---|---|
| J1 | **"Am I okay / going to be okay?"** | Reassurance & orientation |
| J2 | **"Am I behind?"** | Benchmarking against peers/norms ("net worth by age") |
| J3 | **"Am I doing this right?"** | Validation of a decision already half-made |
| J4 | **"Can I afford *this specific thing*?"** | Tradeoff / affordability modeling |
| J5 | **"Something just happened — what do I do *now*?"** | Event navigation (the trigger moments) |
| J6 | **"Am I being taken advantage of / missing something?"** | Defense against fees, scams, bad products, taxes left on the table |

### Layer 2 — The life domains (where the job lands)

Each job expresses itself across a stable set of domains. This is the "table of contents" layer:

- **D1 — Cash flow & budgeting** (paycheck-to-paycheck, where does my money go)
- **D2 — Debt** (cards, student loans, what to pay first)
- **D3 — Saving & emergency resilience** (the $400 question, emergency fund sizing)
- **D4 — Credit** (score, freeze, build, repair)
- **D5 — Investing** (how to start, allocation, am I doing it right)
- **D6 — Retirement** (am I on track, accounts, Social Security timing, decumulation)
- **D7 — Housing** (rent vs. buy, affordability, mortgage)
- **D8 — Income** (make more, side income, salary negotiation)
- **D9 — Taxes** (filing, refund, optimization)
- **D10 — Protection** (insurance, estate, beneficiaries)
- **D11 — Benefits & safety net** (SNAP, Medicaid, subsidies, what am I eligible for)

### Layer 3 — The segment tags (your "modifiers")

These don't get their own section in the taxonomy — they get **applied as tags** to any question, and they change weighting, tone, and stakes:

- **Life stage / age** (Gen Z / Millennial / Gen X / Boomer — see §4)
- **Socioeconomic tier** (subsistence / building / accumulating / decumulating — see §5)
- **Gender** (see §6)
- **Triggering event** (the moment that made them ask *today* — see §7)

**The schema in one line:** every question = `[Job] × [Domain] × [tags: stage, tier, gender, event]`.

Example: "I'm 28, my dad just died and left me his house, what do I do?" =
`J5 (event navigation) × D10 (protection/estate) + D7 (housing)` tagged `stage:Millennial, event:bereavement+inheritance`. The *engine* is the same as "I just got a bonus, what do I do?" (J5 × windfall) — which is why a single Workflow-Assistant-style engine with swappable templates can serve both.

This is the resolution to your dilemma: **don't pick one demographic axis. Build on the jobs+domains grid and let the demographics be filters.**

## 4. Segment deep-dive: by life stage / age

The same question carries radically different stakes by age. The data:

- **Gen Z is in acute crisis and getting worse fast.** Deloitte's 2025 Gen Z & Millennial survey: financial insecurity among Gen Z jumped from **30% (2024) to 48% (2025)** — a 60% spike in one year; Millennials went 32% → 46%. ~40% of Gen Z report stress/anxiety most of the time. Over half live paycheck to paycheck.
- **But the averages hide opposing currents.** Financial Finesse (2026) found Gen Z under 30 earning <$60k actually saw stress *improve* slightly, while Millennials 30–44 earning $60k–$100k saw stress *rise* — the two cancel in aggregate. (Lesson: age × income interaction matters more than age alone — reinforcing the tagging model.)
- **Emergency-fund fragility is a young-adult signature.** BofA Better Money Habits (2025): ~55% of Gen Z and ~49% of Millennials lack 3 months of emergency savings.
- **The wealth gap is generational and structural.** Boomers hold ~one-third of U.S. wealth and ~54% of stocks; Gen Z holds a small fraction despite similar population share (Fortune / Fed data, 2025). The "Great Wealth Transfer" looms but is poorly timed and unevenly distributed.
- **Boomers are the calmest.** They're far less likely to stress about money regularly (NerdWallet/Harris, 2025), and most confident about overcoming bad money habits — but their live questions shift to *decumulation, long-term care, and Social Security timing*.

**Question shift by stage:**
- **Gen Z:** "How do I even start? How do I write a check / build credit / make more money?" (literacy + income)
- **Millennial:** "Am I behind? Can I afford a house / a kid? How do I juggle debt + saving + investing at once?" (the squeeze cohort)
- **Gen X:** "Is it too late? Am I saving enough with college + aging parents bearing down?" (sandwich)
- **Boomer:** "Will it last? When do I claim Social Security? What about long-term care?" (decumulation + protection)

## 5. Segment deep-dive: by socioeconomic tier

This is the dimension most relevant to a financial-equity / underserved-populations product, and it changes the *question itself*, not just the stakes.

A useful four-tier model (by the question being asked, not by income bracket alone):

- **Subsistence ("can I make it to Friday?")** — questions are about *triage and access*: "Which bill do I skip? Am I eligible for SNAP/Medicaid/subsidies? How do I avoid the payday-loan/overdraft trap?" The Fed SHED finds a meaningful share can't cover a $400 shock; ~1 in 4 workers rely on safety-net benefits at some point each year (Performance.gov). Brookings documents that SNAP functions as *de facto* financial-shock insurance, not just food aid. **The hardest problem here isn't literacy — it's navigation of fragmented, demeaning government systems.**
- **Building ("how do I get stable?")** — emergency fund, debt payoff, credit repair, first retirement account. The classic personal-finance-101 ladder.
- **Accumulating ("am I optimizing?")** — investing allocation, tax efficiency, home purchase, employer match.
- **Decumulating ("will it last / how do I draw down?")** — retirement income sequencing, Roth conversions, Social Security timing, long-term-care risk.

**Critical equity finding:** lower-income and minority households face *navigation and access* barriers, not just knowledge gaps — 1 in 10 Black and Hispanic Americans lack a bank account; many rely on costly alternatives (payday/title lenders, pawn) that deepen the cycle (Coalition on Human Needs / Fed data). A tool that *finds eligible benefits and walks someone through claiming them* solves a different (and arguably higher-leverage) problem than one that teaches investing.

## 6. Segment deep-dive: by gender

The gap is real and consistent across surveys:

- **Women report more financial stress.** NerdWallet/Harris (April 2025): **56% of women vs. 45% of men** stress about money regularly. Women are more likely to cite inflation/rising prices as the driver (72% vs. 64%, Bankrate).
- **Women are disproportionately affected by "money avoidance"** — one of the four classic money scripts — leading to lost confidence and forgone opportunity (Psychology Today, 2025). Historical conditioning ("talking money is unfeminine/crass") compounds it.
- **The behavioral consequence, not the math, is the differentiator.** The knowledge gap is smaller than the *confidence and permission* gap. Women's questions more often carry an implicit "is it okay for me to even be asking / deciding this?" subtext.

**Design implication:** gender mostly modifies *tone, framing, and the confidence scaffolding* a tool needs — not the underlying domain. A retirement calculator doesn't change; the surrounding reassurance and "you're allowed to make this call" framing does.

## 7. Segment deep-dive: by triggering event (the "what do I do *now*?" moments)

Events are the highest-intent, highest-stakes, most "I can't Google this with my real name" moments — and they reliably break the assumptions a person's prior plan was built on. The canonical trigger set (synthesized across wealth-planning and consumer sources):

- **Bereavement / death of a spouse or parent** — sudden financial responsibility at peak emotional load; "how do I handle finances when someone dies" is a recurring top Reddit thread.
- **Divorce / separation** — splitting accounts, Social Security/retirement entitlement, rebuilding on one income, updating beneficiaries.
- **Inheritance / windfall** (incl. lottery, business sale, settlement) — paralysis, tax/estate implications, "don't blow it" anxiety.
- **New baby** — insurance, education funds, the ~$310k cost-of-raising-a-child sticker shock.
- **Job loss / job change** — benefits, 401(k) rollover, income gap, identity loss (research notes job loss tied to identity deepens depression).
- **Marriage** — combining finances, joint vs. separate, beneficiary updates.
- **Home purchase / move** — affordability, down payment, mortgage mechanics.
- **Serious illness / disability** — medical cost shock; ~a quarter of Americans skip needed care over cost.
- **Retirement transition** — the shift from accumulation to decumulation.
- **Long-term care need** — often the single largest late-life risk factor.

**Why events are the strongest product wedge:** intent is unambiguous, the person is actively *seeking* (not avoiding), and the question is bounded ("what do I do now") rather than diffuse ("am I okay forever"). Government resources cluster here too (MyMoney.gov organizes its entire site around "Life Events"), which validates the demand but also shows how dry/unhelpful the existing answers are.

## 8. The behavioral layer: why people don't just ask

This is the connective tissue and the reason a *better* tool can win even though information is free and abundant.

- **Money scripts (Klontz).** ~1 in 4 people identify "money avoidance" as their dominant subconscious money belief (Psychology Today, 2025). The four scripts — avoidance, worship, status, vigilance — predict behavior more than income does.
- **Shame archetypes.** "Hiders" avoid opening bills, mute banking notifications, give vague answers; shame produces avoidance which produces worse outcomes which produces more shame — a documented vicious cycle (Decision Fish; ReachLink).
- **The taboo is quantified.** Most-taboo topics to discuss: debt (48%), salary/bonuses (45%), financial struggles (40%) — Zip survey of 16,500 adults, 2024. 71% admit secret spending; 36% have spent $500+ without telling a partner.
- **Feeling >> facts.** Again, the headline behavioral finding: subjective feeling about money matters ~20× more than the actual number for predicting distress. A tool that *only* delivers accurate numbers will underperform one that also resolves the feeling.
- **People want a trusted human, not a search engine.** Empower's framing ("you might seek a good book or a trusted friend") and the Reddit phenomenon (2 in 3 Reddit users have made real financial decisions based on a stranger's comment) both show the unmet need is for a *trusted, attuned voice* — not more PDFs.

## 9. The escalation ladder: where people actually go (and the gap each leaves)

| Channel | Good at | Fails at | The gap it leaves |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Self / avoidance** | Free, no judgment | Avoidance compounds harm | Needs a low-shame *first step* |
| **Friends & family** | Trusted, contextual | Biased, taboo, limited expertise | Want trust *without* social exposure |
| **YouTube / TikTok** | Free, engaging, on-demand | Generic, monetized, can't see *your* numbers | Want personalization, not a generic 10-min video |
| **Reddit / forums** | Anonymous, real situations, fast | Variable quality, no accountability | Want the anonymity *plus* reliability |
| **Google search** | Instant for facts | Drowning in SEO/affiliate content; can't model *your* tradeoff | Want a decision, not 10 blue links |
| **Government / nonprofit (IRS, MyMoney.gov, SNAP)** | Authoritative, free | Dry, fragmented, hard to navigate, demeaning UX | Want *navigation and translation*, not a portal |
| **Paid software (TurboTax, budgeting apps, robo-advisors)** | Structured, does the math | Narrow, transactional, often upsell-driven | Want integrated judgment across domains |
| **Financial advisor** | Personalized, accountable | Expensive, min-asset gated, intimidating, conflicted | Want advisor-grade orientation, accessible to everyone |

**The white space, stated plainly:** people want *the trust of a friend, the anonymity of Reddit, the personalization of an advisor, and the math of software* — in one place, with the shame removed and the feeling addressed alongside the number. No existing channel does all of that.

## 10. Synthesis: sorting questions into "tool-solvable" vs. not

For your downstream step (narrowing to buildable tools), here's a sorting heuristic.

**Strongly tool-solvable** (bounded inputs, deterministic-ish logic, clear output):
- Affordability / tradeoff modeling (J4): "Can I afford X?" / "rent vs. buy" / "should I pay debt or invest"
- Benchmarking (J2): "net worth by age," "am I on track for retirement," "am I behind"
- Event playbooks (J5): structured "what to do now" workflows for death, divorce, windfall, job loss, new baby — *swappable template engine*
- Benefits eligibility & navigation (D11): "what am I eligible for and how do I claim it"
- Optimization defense (J6): "am I leaving tax/fee/match money on the table"

**Partially tool-solvable** (need judgment + reassurance scaffolding, not just math):
- "Am I doing this right?" (J3) — validation needs a trusted-voice layer, not just a calculator
- Retirement decumulation sequencing (D6) — modelable, but the *risk framing* (long-term care) is emotional
- Debt payoff strategy (D2) — math is easy, behavior change is the hard part

**Hard to fully solve with a tool alone** (require human judgment, licensing, or emotional containment):
- Acute crisis triage at subsistence tier (real-time, high-stakes, often needs a human/agency)
- Clinical-adjacent money-disorder territory (avoidance/shame deep enough to need a therapist — a boundary worth keeping bright)
- Anything requiring fiduciary/legal advice (estate documents, tax positions) — tool can *orient and route*, not *advise*

## 11. Where this maps to the build

- **The "question as the front door" principle is vindicated by the data.** The top searches *are* questions, and the emotional jobs (§3) sit right behind them. Front-door = the J×D cell the person lands on.
- **Finxiety** lives mostly in **Layer 1 (the jobs)** and §8 (the behavioral layer) — its differentiator is addressing the *feeling* that the 20× finding says dominates. It's the "trusted friend who came back with real information" archetype made literal.
- **Mortalia** is the deep specialist in **D6 (retirement) × decumulation tier × J1/J4** — the highest-stakes, lowest-reversibility cell, which justifies the premium annual-checkup model and the high build standard.
- **The philanthropy arm** owns **D11 (benefits) × subsistence tier × J5 (events)** — exactly the cell where the gap (§5, §9) is navigation, not literacy, and where a Workflow-Assistant engine with swappable templates is the right architecture.

The jobs+domains grid is a single coordinate system the three products can share — which is the cross-product positioning.
